    Daas (דעת) and Common Sense

    All Men Philosophize

    All men philosophize, whether they know it or not. We behave in certain ways. How we behave is usually guided by many unacknowledged and unarticulated presuppositions we picked up along the way. Our most commonplace expressions of optimism or pessimism, selfishness or high-mindedness, idealism or cynicism, carry along with them unacknowledged assumptions about the nature of the universe as a whole and man’s place in it.

    The most important thing about a man–his mind, his character and his acts–is his view of the universe. It is important for a landlady renting out her house to know what the income of the lodger is, but it is as important to know his philosophy. A businessman will look at the qualifications of a hire, but will the hire be honest? A general about to fight an enemy must know the enemy’s numbers, but it is as important to know the enemy’s philosophy. As I write this we are under attack by a barbaric enemy, an axis of evil (Iran, Hezbollah, Hamas and their allies in the postmodern west). What drives them? Their desire for power and hedonism or militant Islam?

    An outlook or philosophy is no less real because it is unrecognized and hidden in the words we speak the way in which we act. The choice before us, then, is not between accepting or rejecting philosophy, since each of us whether he knows it or not already has one, but between holding it consciously or unconsciously. We are liable to become victims of our own unconsciously held philosophy or of the philosophy of others, which may rule us all the more tyrannically because it is hidden and operates in the dark. What we hold implicitly, vaguely, confusedly, must be rendered explicit, definite, and clear in the light of reason and the evidence of things. So a question we all face is:

    Is the unexamined life worth living?

    Common sense philosophy

    Most men who grow up in a healthy society will have the rudiments of common sense. There are some things that we all share and are common to all of us, simply because we are all human. Our common-sense notions are expressed by such words as “thing”, “body,” “mind,” “nature”, “change, “cause,” “part,” “whole,” “one,” “many,” and so on. We know with certainty that a finite part cannot be greater than the whole. Most of us have been using these words and notions since we were young. We started to use them in order to talk about experiences that are common to all of us of things moving or remaining at rest, of plants growing, of animals being born and dying, of sitting down and getting up, of aches and pains, of making up our minds to be honest or not, of seeking justice or not, and of seeking truth or not.

    It is in this sense that philosophy is everybody’s business. Not only in order to get more information about the world, our society, and ourselves. But common sense philosophy helps us to understand things we already know, but with greater clarity.

    Can philosophy done right refine our common sense?

    Foundation of Daas (דעת)

    The word דעת is hard to translate and it reaches very high in the soul of man. At its foundation is what I shall call common sense realism. The intellectual world of the Rishonim and our earlier Sages was radically different than that which was adopted by the philosophers and scientists of the “Enlightenment”. This is one of the reasons that the writings of Rav Saadia Gaon, the Chovos Halevavos, Kuzari, Moreh Nevuchim and Sefer HaIkrim are rarely studied in our times (at least not comprehensively).

    The Rejection of Plan and Purpose

    A quick summary of the “Enlightenment” as portrayed in our times: Religion is based on blind faith. So the founders of modern Western sought to free science and philosophy from its irrational embrace so as to eliminate its influence from public life, and to indoctrinate students into the new religion of materialistic Scientism.

    The consequence of this is our post-truth post-modern age of irrationality. The moderns had things backward. It would be truer to say that they falsely accused classical thought of resting on blind faith so that they could justify their rejection of it, and they were smart enough to be able to cook up a new conception of what should count as “rational” in the hope of making the accusation stick. They thereby destroyed the intellectual foundations, not only of religion, but of any possible objective morality, and of science itself.

    In an article in The Atlantic Monthly in 1948, the philosopher W.T. Stace (an empiricist who was not himself in sympathy with classical philosophy) said this about the moderns’ decision to abandon that philosophy:

    The real turning point between the medieval age of faith and the modern age of unfaith came when the scientists of the seventeenth century turned their backs upon what used to be called “final causes”. . . [belief in which] was not the invention of Christianity [but] was basic to the whole of Western civilization, whether in the ancient pagan world or in Christendom, from the time of Socrates to the rise of science in the seventeenth century. … They did this on the ground that inquiry into purposes is useless for what science aims at: namely, the prediction and control of events. … The conception of purpose in the world was ignored and frowned upon. This, though silent and almost unnoticed, was the greatest revolution in human history, far outweighing in importance any of the political revolutions whose thunder has reverberated through the world. … The world, according to this new picture, is purposeless, senseless, meaningless. Nature is nothing but matter in motion. The motions of matter are governed, not by any purpose, but by blind forces and laws. … [But] if the scheme of things is purposeless and meaningless, then the life of man is purposeless and meaningless too. Everything is futile, all effort is in the end worthless. A man may, of course, still pursue disconnected ends, money, fame, art, science, and may gain pleasure from them. But his life is hollow at the center. Hence, the dissatisfied, disillusioned, restless, spirit of modern man. … Along with the ruin of the religious vision there went the ruin of moral principles and indeed of all values. … If our moral rules do not proceed from something outside us in the nature of the universe – whether we say it is G-d or simply the universe itself – then they must be our own inventions. Thus it came to be believed that moral rules must be merely an expression of our own likes and dislikes. But likes and dislikes are notoriously variable. What pleases one man, people or culture, displeases another. Therefore, morals are wholly relative.

    Plan and Purpose

    But do we not see that the purpose of the eyes are to see and the purpose of the heart is to pump blood. On what basis did these men of “enlightenment” reject a cosmos buzzing with plan and purpose? On no basis whatsoever!

    The philosopher and historian of science E.A. Burtt, in his classic study of The Metaphysical Foundations of Modern Physical Science (p305 to 306), writes that “wishful thinking” lay behind the belief of the philosophers of the “Enlightenment” (basing themselves on Hume, Kant etc.) that all the phenomena previously explained in Aristotelian terms could be readily accommodated by a new science in which formal and final causes were replaced by entirely quantifiable properties of the kind suitable for prediction and control of nature, and subsequent technological application.

    “The sources of distraction [from this ambition] simply had to be denied or removed, … and any solution of the ultimate questions which continued to pop up, however superficial and inconsistent, that served to quiet the situation, to give a tolerably plausible response to their questionings in the categories they were now familiar with, and above all to open before them a free field for their fuller mathematical exploitation of nature, tended to be readily accepted and tucked away in their minds with uncritical confidence.”

    Burtt wrote this even though he was sympathetic to the modern project. His statements parallels the acknowledgment by more contemporary writers like Nagel and Lewontin that the naturalistic interpretation of science to which they are committed is less an independently motivated philosophical position than an attempt to prevent a “divine foot” from getting in the door. The rejection of the proofs for the existence and Unity of G-d is based on wishful thinking and errors made by some very smart early moderns.

    The corruptions of daas in the modern era is profound. It is that light that I say there is a need to return to the intellectual world of the Rishonim.
